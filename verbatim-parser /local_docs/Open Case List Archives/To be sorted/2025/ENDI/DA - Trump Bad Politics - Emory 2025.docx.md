The Polar Security Cutter Design Is A Problem:

It is easy to forget the Polar Security Cutter Program was originally
meant to deliver an entirely new class of heavy polar icebreakers by
2024. With a lot of fanfare, the Coast Guard awarded a three ship
contract in 2019, and then swiftly discovered\--as I warned six years
ago\--that the shipyard's engineering capabilities were too weak to
survive both the Coast Guard's aggressive schedule and the shipyard's
low, bargain-basement bid of \$1.9 billion.

Today, taxpayers have little to celebrate beyond a catalogue of
inexplicable missteps, basic planning failures and organizational
disruption. Parts of the shipyard had to be redeveloped because planners
failed to understand the vessel's ultimate weight. Naval architects
somehow overlooked the design of an entire key deck, a debacle that
likely led the original bidder, a subsidiary of Singapore-based
conglomerate ST Engineering, to sell the yard in 2022 fire-sale.
Production facilities and processes were---and apparently still
are---struggling to handle icebreaker-caliber steel.

The laundry list of basic engineering oversights and production mistakes
offers little confidence that the PSC will be anything more than a
flawed and unreliable vessel.

But, rather than halt the program, or simply force the contractors to
focus on complying with the Government Accountability Office's set of
best shipbuilding processes, both the Department of Homeland Security
and the Coast Guard have consistently doubled down on building up the
program's inevitability. Nothing\--no matter how egregious the
mistake\--seems to stick on this teflon ship. The PSC just keeps
rumbling along, lurching from one misstep to another.

The programmatic momentum was on full display in December 2024. In the
final days of the Biden Administration, the Coast Guard quietly approved
construction of the first PSC and, with DOGE absent, the Program is
anticipating a larger production decision sometime in the next few
months

Authorization to construct the ship was unnecessary. Aside from the fact
that the shipyard is still struggling to complete several "practice"
modules of the ship\--whose construction was authorized a year and a
half ago, the formal authorization to begin PSC-1 construction was in
defiance of best shipbuilding practices.

This is a serious issue. The Coast Guard says that the "current design
maturity for the whole ship is 90% for functional design, and 72% for
overall design maturity." That's not great. Government watchdogs at the
U.S. Government Accountability Office want 100% of the functional design
complete before construction starts. That's tough to do, but the numbers
are in the GAO's favor. According to the GAO, the Coast Guard's decision
to start building the Offshore Patrol Cutter when the functional design
was 97% complete "led to construction rework and contributed to an
estimated cost increase of 19 percent to the lead ship" and "a schedule
delay of the lead Offshore Patrol Cutter by almost 2 years." The first
OPC has yet to be delivered, and the costs are likely to increase. If
the PSC aligns with the OPC's ugly performance trends, starting PSC
construction with this amount of design uncertainty puts the Coast Guard
at real risk of an even bigger fiasco.

Admittedly, naval architecture is a challenge for everybody on the
waterfront. Since 2022, the Coast Guard and their "new" PSC contractor,
Bollinger Shipyards, have worked hard to find naval architects who have
the right skills needed to help move the design maturation process
forward. But increased speed towards a goal shouldn't be a surrogate for
completion of the goal itself. Things might be going better for the PSC
right now, but...better progress shouldn't hide the ugly fact that, six
years after contract award, the PSC design isn't done, and checks on the
work already done still need to be brought to completion.

Worryingly, the transitional design, a point when the ship's 2D
functional design drawings are translated into sort of a walk-through 3D
model, appears to be lagging behind, at around 60% complete. But that 3D
model is where the rubber really hits the road. A 3D model helps reveal
systemic problems with major distributed ship systems (water, power and
HVAC, for example), and identify alignment issues that don't reveal
themselves easily on 2D drawings. It helps the builder generate the
final production design, which, in turn, informs schedule and price.

That's a concern. Nothing should get built until the 3D model is
complete. On February 19, at a Hudson Institute meeting, Nickolas H.
Guertin, the former Assistant Secretary of the Navy for Research,
Development and Acquisition, emphasized the importance of a stable
design, saying, "it would be reasonable to say that you need to have at
least the 3D model part of it done sufficiently so that you know what
you're building," refining it "down to a high enough level of detail so
that you're not incurring/discovering risk in the construction process."
In his words, government shipbuilders should "cheat a little" by using a
completed ship design to drive down risk and increase the chance a new
ship can be built profitably. That's not been done with the Polar
Security Cutter. In fact, the Coast Guard has quietly been building the

After the original bidder for the Polar Security Cutter program quit,
