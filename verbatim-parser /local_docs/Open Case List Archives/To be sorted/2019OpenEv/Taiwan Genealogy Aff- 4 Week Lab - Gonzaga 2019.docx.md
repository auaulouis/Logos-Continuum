#### Just days after his election, Donald Trump received an historic telephone call from Tsai Ing-wen, the President of Taiwan, marking the first time in nearly 40 years that an American president has spoken directly with the leader of the ROC. Many celebrated the call as bold support for the democratic people of Taiwan against the "brutal" tyranny of China. Others criticized it for threatening to undermine America's delicate relationship and ratchet up economic and military tensions. This controversy has only been magnified by the Trump administration's recent approval of the sale of billions of dollars' worth of tanks, missiles and other military equipment to the ROC.

#### Revealingly, neither side of this debate thought it signaled anything like support for true Taiwanese independence or statehood. Taiwan exists in a permanent state of exception. Not subject to Chinese rule -- not a nation-state of its own. Taiwan demonstrates the crisis of modern sovereignty, and its people are the "bare life" of modern biopolitics.

#### To understand Taiwan's unusual position, it's not enough to look at current policy. The roots of Taiwan's exceptional status are not new. The island has changed hands between colonizer after colonizer since at least the 1600s. Its current situation, however, can be marked by America's military victory in World War II and the onset of the Cold War. 

#### In August 1950, just weeks after US troops arrived on the Korean peninsula ready to fight the threat of communism in Asia, General Douglas MacArthur, Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers and functional colonial Governor of post-War Japan, delivered a speech outlining the beginnings of a significant evolution in America's understanding of its "western strategic frontier." No longer limited to the west coast of the continental US, where "menacing avenues of approach" were open to "any predatory force" out to get us, "all of this was changed by our Pacific victory." MacArthur proclaimed, "Our strategic frontier ... shifted to embrace the entire Pacific Ocean, which has become a vast moat to protect us as long as we hold it... It acts as a protective shield to all of the Americas and all free lands ... we control."

#### MacArthur's speech made clear the emerging strategic attitude toward this new "frontier." America was in the process of transforming the threatening vastness of the Pacific into a theatre of militarized control. At the core of MacArthur's argument was the strategic centrality of an unusual military asset -- an "*unsinkable aircraft carrier*" that, if lost, would neutralize our entire Pacific defense system and expose all "free nations of the Pacific basin" to inevitable "war of conquest."

#### America's "unsinkable aircraft carrier" was no typical military warship, but rather the perfectly situated island of Taiwan. Not a people. Not a state. But a weapon.
